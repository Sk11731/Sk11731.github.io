Addressing Mass Unemployment Due to AI Automation

A comparative policy analysis weighing AI-literacy programs, hybrid-role tax incentives, and workforce reskilling — concluding that reskilling is the only response built to outlast the disruption it's meant to solve.

DateJune 2026
SkillsPolicy Analysis, Research
RoleCo-Author
01 — The Problem

Displacement is outpacing the systems built to absorb it

AI automation is performing advanced cognitive work, from legal analysis to financial forecasting. The result is a widening skill gap. The ILO estimates 2 billion workers are in informal employment globally with no structural access to retraining.

02 — Methodology

A three-criteria evaluation framework

Drawing on OECD and World Economic Forum data, we test three policies:

  • Scalability — Reaching millions without proportional cost.
  • Implementation speed — Acting while displacement is active.
  • Economic impact — Moving people into durable, high-wage roles.
03 — Key Findings

AI Literacy

Fast and cheap, but informs rather than re-employs. Fails to help the fully automated.

Hybrid Incentives

Preserves jobs short-term but excludes informal workers. Never closes the underlying skill gap.

Reskilling

Slower to stand up, but the only option moving workers into genuine, durable demand.

04 — Recommendation

Government-funded reskilling

Workforce reskilling is the recommended policy. It is the only option built to outlast the disruption, moving displaced workers into growing sectors rather than buying time around a shrinking one.
